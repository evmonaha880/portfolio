Just Build More Boxes

The glut, the monopoly, and the deficit, and why a shipping container is a natural fit for an AI agent.

When the system couldn’t get boxes to the right place, it didn’t get smarter. It just built more. China’s container factories ran flat out, the global box pool ballooned, and within about eighteen months the shortage flipped into a glut of millions of idle containers rotting in depots. The industry’s answer to a coordination problem was a manufacturing problem.

You cannot manufacture your way out of a problem that is really about knowing where things are and acting on it.

Who actually makes the boxes

This surprises everyone, including supply-chain people: container manufacturing is one of the most concentrated industries on earth, and it is almost entirely in one country.

  • Virtually 100% of the world’s shipping containers are built in China. Drewry puts Chinese factories at 96%+ of dry cargo containers and 100% of reefers.
  • Three companies dominate. In Q1 2021 the top three Chinese builders made 82% of global production: CIMC (~42%, the largest in the world), Dong Fang International, a COSCO unit (~26%), and CXIC Group (~14%).
  • CIMC is state-controlled: the US Department of Commerce concluded its operations are ultimately controlled by the Chinese government. In 2021 CIMC bought Maersk’s container-manufacturing arm, removing one of the last non-Chinese producers.

The structural fact is that the supply of the asset itself is governed by a factory throttle, build more or build less, rather than by where boxes are actually needed. There is no real elasticity.

The whiplash

A three-act arc, all inside about twenty-four months.

Act 1, Shortage (2020–21). COVID demand surged, boxes got stranded at congested ports, carriers couldn’t get equipment where it was needed, and rates spiked so hard that carriers could charge a premium just to guarantee a box.

Act 2, Panic-build (2021–22). Carriers responded the only way the system knows how: order more. Production hit records, 7.1M TEU built in 2021 and 3.8M in 2022. The global pool jumped ~13% to nearly 50M TEU, roughly three times the normal growth trend, even as trade volumes barely moved.

Act 3, Glut (2022–23). Demand softened and the new boxes had nowhere to go. A surplus of ~6M TEU of idle containers piled up; secondhand box prices collapsed ~79% year-over-year. New production cratered to 1.3–1.5M TEU in 2023, down from 7.1M just two years prior. Depots literally ran out of space to store the empties.

7.1M → 3.8M → 1.4M TEU built, three years running. The industry slammed the accelerator, then the brake, and got rich-then-broke whiplash, because it was steering by a rear-view mirror.

What the trade deficit has to do with all of it

A container is a one-way vehicle for a two-way accounting problem. The US runs a massive goods trade deficit with China; it imports far more than it exports back. In 2020 the US imported ~$435B from China and exported only ~$125B the other way. So follow a single box:

  • It arrives on the West Coast full of Chinese goods. Earning.
  • It’s unloaded. Now the US needs to send something back in it, but it doesn’t export enough physical goods to China to fill all those boxes.
  • The box is now a liability sitting on the wrong continent: ship it back empty (pay to haul air), or let it sit (pay storage and detention).
  • Either way, the trade imbalance has converted a productive asset into a cost. Sixty million times a year.

And it nests. Boxes land on the West Coast (LA/Long Beach is ~40% of US imports), get railed inland to deliver goods, then strand in the interior or on the East Coast, far from the ports where they’re needed to refill. The deficit creates the macro one-way flow; geography creates a second one-way flow inside it.

Why this is the killer frame

The trade deficit means the “right place” for a box is constantly, structurally moving away from where boxes accumulate. It is a permanent, large-scale, real-time imbalance. You cannot fix it by building more boxes, which just adds liabilities to the surplus side, and you cannot fix it with a human planner, because the board is too big and changes too fast. It is, definitionally, a continuous global optimization-and-action problem.

The missing capability isn’t more steel. It’s distributed intelligence that knows where every box is, where it’s needed, and can act on the gap continuously.

That is why a shipping container, of all things, is where the case for an AI agent is clearest: a permanent, real-time placement problem that no factory and no human planner can keep up with. The answer was never more boxes. It was better routing of the ones we already have.
